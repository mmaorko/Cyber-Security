# definitions of Security Principles, Frameworks, and Governance
## Asset: 
Anything that has value to an organization. This can include data, systems, hardware, software, or even reputation.

## Availability: 
Ensuring that systems and data are accessible to authorized users whenever they are needed.

## Compliance: 
Following internal policies as well as external laws, regulations, and industry standards.

## Confidentiality: 
Making sure that information is only accessible to people who are properly authorized.

## Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability (CIA) Triad: 
A foundational security model that focuses on protecting data through three core principles—keeping it private, accurate, and accessible.

## Hacktivist: 
An individual who uses hacking techniques to promote political or social causes.

## Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA):
A U.S. law designed to protect the privacy and security of patients’ medical information.

## Integrity: 
Maintaining the accuracy, consistency, and trustworthiness of data throughout its lifecycle.

## National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Cybersecurity Framework (CSF): 
A widely used framework that provides standards, guidelines, and best practices for managing and reducing cybersecurity risk.

## Privacy Protection: 
The practice of safeguarding personal data from unauthorized access, misuse, or disclosure.

## Protected Health Information (PHI): 
Any health-related information that can be linked to a specific individual, including medical history and treatment details.

## Security Architecture: 
The structured design of security systems, tools, and processes that work together to protect an organization from threats.

## Security Controls: 
pecific measures or safeguards implemented to reduce or eliminate security risks.

## Security Ethics: 
Professional principles that guide responsible and lawful behavior in cybersecurity roles.

## Security Frameworks:
Structured guidelines and best practices used to design and improve an organization’s security posture.

## Security Governance: 
The leadership, policies, and processes that define how security is managed and enforced within an organization.

## Sensitive Personally Identifiable Information (SPII): 
A category of personal data that requires stricter protection due to its sensitive nature, such as Social Security numbers or financial information.
