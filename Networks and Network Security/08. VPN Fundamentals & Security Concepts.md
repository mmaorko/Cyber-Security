# VPN
A VPN is a security tool that creates a private, encrypted "tunnel" over a public network (like the internet). It allows a user to send and receive data as if their 
computing device were directly connected to a private network, even when they are miles away.

## How it Works 
To understand a VPN like a security analyst, you need to look at three specific processes:

- **_Encapsulation:_** The VPN takes your data packet and wraps it inside another packet. This hides your original data (like your browsing history or DNS requests) from your
Internet Service Provider (ISP) and hackers.

- **_Encryption:_** It locks the data inside that tunnel using a mathematical algorithm (like AES-256). Even if a malicious actor intercepts the data, they cannot read it
without the unique "key" held by your device and the VPN server.

- **_IP Masking:_** Your real IP address is replaced by the IP address of the VPN server. To any website you visit, it looks like you are browsing from the server's
location (e.g., London or New York) rather than your actual physical location.

## Encapsulation
Packet encapsulation is the most important technology of a VPN.

- **_Problem:_** If you encrypt the entire data packet, routers will not be able to understand where to send it (because the IP and MAC addresses are also encrypted).
- **_Solution (Encapsulation):_** A VPN places your original (encrypted) packet inside a new “outer” packet.
- **_Inner Packet:_** Contains your original data and real IP address (which is encrypted and unreadable to attackers).
- **_Outer Packet:_** Contains the VPN server’s IP address, which routers can read and use to deliver the packet to the correct destination.

## The Real Problem
Many people think that using a VPN means 100% security. Remember, a VPN only secures the path between you and the VPN server. If you download malware yourself or 
click on a phishing link, a VPN will not be able to protect you.

## Encryption
Encryption is a digital lock for your information. It converts readable text (plaintext) into an unreadable code (ciphertext) using an algorithm, which cannot be understood 
without the correct key.

### How Encryption Works
Encryption mainly depends on two things:

- **_Algorithm (The Cipher):_** This is a mathematical formula (such as AES, RSA). It defines how the data will be scrambled.

- **_Key:_** This is a secret string or password. In modern encryption, the algorithm is not kept secret (it can be open source), but the key must be kept highly confidential.

### What type of encryption use in VPN
VPNs typically use a strong combination of both Symmetric and Asymmetric encryption. This is known as Hybrid Encryption.

#### 1. Symmetric Encryption (Secret Key)
This is the fastest and oldest form of encryption. It uses the same single key to both lock (encrypt) and unlock (decrypt) the data.

- **_How it works:_** Think of a physical safe. You use one specific key to lock it, and you must give that exact same key to your friend so they can open it.
- **_Pros:_** Extremely fast; ideal for encrypting large amounts of data (like a hard drive or a VPN tunnel).
- **_Cons:_** The "Key Exchange Problem"—if a hacker steals the key while you are sending it to your partner, they can unlock all your data.
- **_Common Algorithm:_** AES (Advanced Encryption Standard).

#### 2. Asymmetric Encryption (Public Key)
This is the foundation of modern internet security. It uses a pair of mathematically linked keys: a Public Key and a Private Key.
- **_How it works:_** Think of a mailbox. Anyone can drop a letter through the slot (Public Key), but only the owner has the physical key to open the back and read the letters (Private Key).
- **_Pros:_** Highly secure because you never have to share your "unlocking" key with anyone.
- **_Cons:_** Computationally heavy and much slower than symmetric encryption.
- **_Common Algorithms:_** RSA, ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography). This is what makes HTTPS possible.

### 3. Hashing – Data Integrity
Technically, hashing is not encryption, because it cannot be reversed (decrypted) back to its original form. However, it is essential for security.

- **_How it works:_**
It converts data of any size into a fixed-length code. Even if a single comma ( , ) or space is changed in the data, the entire hash value will change.

- **_Purpose:_**
To verify data integrity. For example, storing passwords or checking whether a file has been modified.

- **_Examples:_**
SHA-256, MD5

### **_The Real Problem:_**
Even though encryption is strong, the absence of Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS) can be a major risk. PFS ensures that a unique key is generated for each session. Even if an attacker 
somehow obtains the key of one session, they cannot read data from past or future sessions.


## IP Masking – Hiding Your Digital Identity

IP Masking is a security technique that hides your device’s real public IP address (which can reveal your physical location and ISP information) by using an alternative IP

### How IP Masking Works
Normally, when you visit a website, your packet goes directly to the server, and the server can see your real IP. In IP masking, an intermediary server is used:

- Your request first goes from your device to a masking server (such as a VPN).
- The server replaces your real IP in the packet’s “Source IP” field with its own IP.
- The destination server sees the request as coming from the VPN server, not from you.

### The Real Problem
Even after IP masking, IP leaks can still occur. If your VPN connection suddenly drops, your device may automatically reconnect to the internet using your real IP. 
This can expose your masking or disguise.

- **_Solution:_** In professional settings, always keep the VPN Kill Switch enabled. This ensures that if the VPN tunnel disconnects for any reason, the entire internet
connection is cut off, preventing your real IP from leaking.

- **_Common Mistake:_** Many people think IP masking makes them 100% invisible online. Remember, even if your IP is masked, if you are logged into personal
accounts (such as Gmail), Google still knows who you are.

- **_Tips:_**
During network investigations, if you notice a user frequently changing their IP but their MAC address remains the same, it indicates that IP masking or a proxy is being used.
